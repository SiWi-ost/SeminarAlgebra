The decidability of systems of multivariate polynomial equations depends on the context in which they are considered. Here’s a breakdown of the key aspects:

1. Over the Complex Numbers (ℂ)

  • Hilbert’s Nullstellensatz (a foundational result in algebraic geometry) connects systems of polynomial equations over ℂ to ideals in polynomial rings.
  • Decidability: The problem of determining whether a system of polynomial equations over ℂ has a solution is undecidable in general. This is a consequence of the unsolvability of the halting problem and the fact that polynomial equations can encode arbitrary computations.
  • Special Cases: For specific classes of systems (e.g., linear systems, quadratic systems with certain constraints), decidability can sometimes be achieved using Gröbner bases or other algebraic methods.

2. Over the Real Numbers (ℝ)

  • Tarski’s Theorem: The first-order theory of the real numbers with addition and multiplication (i.e., real closed fields) is decidable. This means that the problem of determining whether a system of polynomial equations and inequalities over ℝ has a solution is decidable.
  • Algorithms: Tools like cylindrical algebraic decomposition (CAD) can be used to decide the satisfiability of such systems.
  • Complexity: While decidable, the problem is computationally expensive, with double-exponential time complexity in the worst case.

3. Over the Integers (ℤ)

  • Hilbert’s 10th Problem: This problem asked whether there exists an algorithm to determine whether a system of polynomial equations with integer coefficients has a solution in integers. The answer is no: the problem is undecidable (proven by Matiyasevich, building on work by Davis, Putnam, and Robinson).
  • Implications: This means that even for relatively simple polynomial equations (e.g., quadratic forms), there is no general algorithm to decide solvability over ℤ.

4. Over Finite Fields (e.g., ℤ/pℤ)

  • For systems of polynomial equations over a finite field, the problem is decidable because:
    • The field is finite, so the number of possible solutions is finite.
    • Algorithms like Gröbner bases can be used to compute the solution set explicitly.
  • Complexity: While decidable, the problem can still be computationally intensive for large fields or high-degree polynomials.

5. Over the Rationals (ℚ)

  • The problem of deciding whether a system of polynomial equations has a solution over ℚ is undecidable in general. This follows from the undecidability over ℤ, as ℚ can be embedded into ℤ via scaling.
  • Special Cases: For specific classes of systems (e.g., linear systems), decidability can be achieved.

Key Takeaways

  • Undecidable in general: Over ℂ, ℤ, and ℚ, the problem is undecidable due to deep connections with computation and logic.
  • Decidable in special cases: Over ℝ and finite fields, the problem is decidable, though computationally challenging in practice.
  • Practical implications: For most real-world applications, researchers rely on heuristic methods, numerical approximations, or specialized algorithms for subclasses of polynomial systems.
